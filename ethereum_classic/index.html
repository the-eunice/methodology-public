Ethereum Classic (ETC)

Ethereum Classic project came into existence back in 2016 when a number of members of the Ethereum community refused the hard fork due to the ideological principle that the blockchain cannot be changed, therefore decided to keep using the un-forked version of Ethereum. With that said, you can consider Ethereum Classic as original Ethereum, whereas current Ethereum is changed version.

Electricity Consumption

Unfortunately we cannot use digiconomist to crawl energy consumption data for Ethereum Classic - so we have to do a bit more of a calculation to get the energy consumption figures.
By checking 2cryptocalc [4], we can see all GPUs have the same hash rate for solving ethash or etchash. Because of this, we can probably fairly assume that the same distribution of GPUs which are used to mine Ethereum are probably used for Ethereum Classic. We can use both of these findings to try and simplify our calculations of energy consumption since data for Ethereum is much more widespread than Ethereum Classic.
We will then use from Ethereum (because of above):
  • The same mining GPU hash rates
  • The same mining GPU distribution (of models)
  • The same mining GPU hash efficiency (i.e., hash/s/W)
In order to calculate energy consumption, we are effectively dividing the daily hash rate (H/s) by the hash rate efficiency (H/s/w).
In order to get the hash rate efficiency we do the following:
  1. Using another studies’ bottom up approach to calculating Ethereum emissions [5]. We can source typical hash rates for different mining rigs and their power requirement ethereum_classic/loaders/data/benchmark.csv
  2. We can then get data on the most popular mining rigs from HiveOS [6] to get the distribution of mining rigs being used to mine Ethereum Classic.[a]. We also get the most popular mining rigs on f2pool - this includes some ASIC mining rigs; and ViaBTC which also includes some ASIC rigs. Since we do not have a percentage used for ViaBTC and F2Pool, we assume an even distribution for all the popular mining rigs.
  3. Using the distribution (i.e., the share of mining done on each rig or even distribution on f2pool), their respective hash rates and powers; we are able to calculate the hash efficiency and multiply this by the share. Summing this we can get an overall weighted hash rate efficiency for mining Ethereum Classic
  4. We use a mean between the overall weighted hash rate efficiency on HiveOS, F2Pool and ViaBTC.
  5. We can then divide the daily average network hash rate by the hash rate efficiency to get the power required to achieve the hash rate.

Notes on methodology for energy consumption

  • For simplicity (for now) we have ignored the date at which GPUs/mining rigs were released. It’s very likely that hash efficiency was lower in the years preceding. Since better equipment more recently released is likely to be more efficient.
  • The HiveOS statistics on Popular Mining Rigs does not actually provide a share of ASIC rigs being used. However, we use a mean between HiveOS, ViaBTC and F2Pool - so I expect we would get a reasonably fair estimation of hash rate efficiency
  • This approach is different to digiconomist, and I would expect (as outlined in the approach I followed [5] [7]) the energy consumption to be lower and different if we used this same approach for Ethereum (and compared against digiconomist’s values).
  • Data center power usage effectiveness - we have elected to use a figure of 1.1 for this which matches CBECI’s best guess estimate [8].
  • Power Supply Efficiency - We have elected a rough figure of 90% PSU [9].
  • We have not considered profitability in our estimates (the assumption that miners will run equipment as long as it remains profitable in electricity terms). This is the key assumption in CBECI and digiconomist’s estimations of power consumption.
