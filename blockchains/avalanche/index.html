Avalanche (AVAX) Carbon Footprint Methodology

The emissions due to holding and transacting Bitcoin (BTC) are calculated using the methodology outlined here, with the following modifications, data sources, and assumptions.

Electricity Consumption

The Avalanche documentation provides the minimum resource requirements for an Avalanche node:
  • Equivalent of 8 AWS vCPU
  • 16 GB RAM
  • 1 TB Storage
Using AWS data for EC2 Instances and taking the average of those with 8vCPU and 16 GB RAM, we get a power requirement of 47W.
Since we have the historical validator count - we can combine this data with our power consumption figure to calculate energy consumption data for historical dates.
The CCRI analysis estimates Avalanche’s energy consumption to be 489,311 kWh per year. Our data gives an average energy consumption of 572,128 kWh per year. This is in the right ballpark, and the discrepancy is probably a result of us taking a more conservative energy estimate.

Carbon emissions

The geographic distribution of validator nodes is available from Avascan.
Given this geographic breakdown, we use emissions factors for grid-purchased electricity in the various countries to calculate the total carbon emissions due to the electricity consumption of the Avalanche network.
The emissions factors for grid-purchased electricity in the respective countries come from various national and multi-national government sources including the US EPA, and the European AIB, as well as climate NGOs such as Greenhouse Gas Protocol, and Climate Transparency.

Blockchain Metrics

  • Daily circulating supply from Messari
  • Daily transaction count and fees from the Avax metrics API
  • Staking rewards from the Avax staking rewards web page
  • Validator geographic distribution from Avascan
  • Daily validator count from the Avax metrics API
We have included the validator count not only from the three platform subnets (C-Chain, P-Chain and X-Chain) but also the subnets on which other blockchains are deployed.
We have made this decision as these validators still contribute to the network and contribute to Avalanche emissions.
Furthermore, since Avalanche is a Layer 0 chain - i.e. it allows other protocols (subnets) to be built on Avalanche - it makes sense to include these in our emission analysis.

Assumptions and limitations

  • We are assuming geographic distribution of validator nodes before we started gathering daily data was the same as at the point when we started
  • We have not included embedded carbon of the node hardware in our analysis
