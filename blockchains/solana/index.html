Solana Carbon Footprint Methodology

  • Solana is a Layer 1 protocol which boasts higher transaction throughput compared to Ethereum
  • Solana uses Proof of Stake (PoS) plus Proof of History (PoH)
  • Proof of History is a way of syncing time across a distributed system (i.e. validator nodes on the Solana protocol) and allows the higher transaction throughput
  • Proof of History operates before consensus (the Proof of Stake step)

Solana PoS and PoH Explanation:

  1. Solana Proof of Stake works by one lead validator processing transactions at any one time and updating network state
  2. Other validators then verify and vote to confirm this
  3. To maximise efficiency, Solana splits the ledger into small chunks and elects a different validator to be the leader each time (roughly every 400ms)
  4. This schedule to choose leaders is pre-determined and randomly generated, according to how much they have staked and previous transactions
Proof of history is used to ensure:
  • Validators trade off leadership at the right time
  • No malicious validators try to skip order

Electricity Consumption

There are two relevant node types on the Solana network; validator nodes and RPC nodes.
The Solana team published the Solana Carbon Footprint Analysis spreadsheet which includes a figure of 984W for validator node power consumption, based on the hardware requirements for a validator node.
Although they do not publish a similar figure for RPC nodes, they do provide details of the hardware requirements for an RPC node. Using these we can calculate the power requirements for an RPC node, which works out to 1,034W.

Daily Transaction Fees

There are two transaction types:
  • Non-vote transactions - these are actual normal transactions like A sending some SOL to B
  • Vote transactions - these are transactions between validators to vote and confirm network state (e.g. confirm transactions)
Both vote and non-vote transactions attract a transaction fee. This is because vote transactions are sent as normal system transfers, hence validators even when voting must send a transaction fee.
To fairly calculate emissions per transaction we have included both of these types of transactions, since both are required to maintain the network.

Daily Staking Rewards

We fetch staking rewards from the official Solana API which is an RPC node on the Solana network.
In order to get all staking rewards passed to these vote accounts we do the following:
  1. Get all program accounts tied to the Solana stake program ID (Stake11111111111111111111111111111111111111)
  2. Get inflation reward for each address fetched from 1
  3. The above returns the inflation reward per epoch[^1], so we fetch the block time associated with the inflation reward
  4. Get the UNIX time associated with the epoch
  5. We now have a staking reward schedule with actual timestamps for validators on the network
  6. We then split the rewards distributed within the epoch to a daily level to allow us to calculate emissions related to staking on a daily basis

  • Transaction and fee information is provided by Solscan
  • Staking rewards data comes from the Solana API
  • Validator information comes from validators.app and Solanabeach
  • Data prior to August 2022 comes from Dune Analytics, Solscan, and Messari

Assumptions and limitations

  • Carbon footprint calculations for dates before August 2022 assume the same geographic distribution as on 2022-08-01, since historical data is not available
  • Prior to August 2022, we assume the same validator/RPC node type split as on 2022-08-01

Comparison with Solana Carbon Footprint Calculation

The Solana team published the Solana Carbon Footprint Analysis. However, there are some issues with their estimate:
  • They are mix and matching carbon accounting approaches. They have highlighted their two biggest data centres (accounting for 27% of all validator accounts) as having 0 gCO2/kWh emissions intensity because they use renewable energy. However, this ‘renewable’ energy is via the European Energy Certificate System (EECS), which is analogous to carbon offsets in the sense that the actual electricity supply is the national electricity grid, and hence we believe the zero emissions figure is not realistic.
  • They have also used the power output for their validators as 984 W, and do not account for the higher power requirements of RPC nodes.
  • The Solana methodology has used a figure of 20,000,000,000 transactions per year
  • Solana has used some emissions factors from 2020 and earlier to do their analysis for 2022
Solana estimates emissions of 2,976 tonnes CO2 per year.
Our analysis estimates emissions of 5,214 tonnes of CO2 per year.
Solana estimates 15,036,276 kWh per year energy consumption.
Our analysis estimates 17,258,514 kwH per year energy consumption.
[^1]: An epoch is a length of time for which a leader schedule is valid (i.e. the order in which a leader is selected to process transactions every 400ms). At a certain time the epoch is invalid and a new one will be randomly generated. At this time, the staking rewards will be given to all validators who validated transactions during the previous epoch. An epoch is roughly between 2 and 3 days on Solana.
