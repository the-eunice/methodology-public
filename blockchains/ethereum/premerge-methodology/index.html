Ethereum (ETH) Carbon Footprint Methodology

Platform Emissions

Electricity Consumption

Since the Merge (migration of Ethereum to PoS) has now happened (15th September 2022). We no longer need to keep track of mining rig hashrate efficiencies to calculate energy consumption. Therefore, we can use already present historical data rather than using a loader (as is the case for Ethereum Classic).
Using another study’s bottom up approach of calculating emissions for Ethereum, we have a graph which plots historical hashrate efficiency against release date of mining rigs. This should typically show us how hashrate efficiency has improved over time and since mining Ethereum should use a spread of both the most efficient and least efficient - we can create a trend line over time including all these data points to get this data at different points in time. We can then use this estimate to get the energy consumption by dividing the network hashrate by this hashrate efficiency best-guess.
Other constants used in the calculation for energy consumption:
  • Data center power usage effectiveness - we have elected to use a figure of 1.1 for this which matches CBECI’s best guess estimate.
  • Power Supply Efficiency - We have elected a rough figure of 90% PSU from another studies’ approach to calculating emissions for Ethereum.

Geographic Distribution

We use the following process to estimate hashrate distribution:
  1. Load pool hashrates directly from either a mining pool’s website or API.
  2. Allocate the mining pools hashrates to different regions.
  3. Estimate the average hashrate of each region during the day.
  4. Use daily average hashrate of each region to determine estimate the hashrate share of each region.
We only focus on the following mining pools for now as together the roughly equate to 70% of hashing power. They are also fairly stable in terms of taking the leading positions.

Since there is no way for us to know the hashrate share of each region for f2pool, we assume an even distribution for now.
poolin
API does not offer overall hashrate or geolocation. We also cannot use their API without an account. We crawl their overall hashrate from their website: poolin.
After June 2021, they moved to Texas US and built their mining farm there according to their website and this poolin official video. We will use Texas to represent all their hashrate distribution. Before June 2021, it is reasonable to assume poolin mainly operates in China Mainland.
hiveon
API offers overall hashrate and server regions.
For locations, they operate in three regions: RU, EU, NAW. Without further details, we assume the following mapping:
{"ru": "Russian_Federation", "eu": "Europe", "naw": "United_States"}
Without further details, we assume its hashrate has an even distribution across all operate regions: 33% each.

There is no geolocation associated to hashrate, but it discloses the regions: asia, eu, us. Their ETH mining pool page shows all the available nodes.
If we hover over the node name it should have a popup with location information our best guess is that they operate in the following regions:
  • US_Florida

  • France

Since it is not possible to distinguish the hashrate shared between these regions, we assume it’s an even distribution.
flexpool
flexpool offers real-time, 24h average and historical hashrate distribution via the flexpool API:
Their servers are located in the following regions while their statistics combine some locations together and report in only [5 aggregated regions]((https://www.flexpool.io/en-GB/statistics):
  regions_mapping = {
      "regions.as": ["Hong_Kong", "Singapore", "South_Korea"],
      "regions.au": ["Australia"],
      "regions.eu": ["Germany", "Sweden"],
      "regions.sa": ["Brazil"],
      "regions.us": ["US_Oregon", "US_Virginia"],
  }
regions.us has a general separation of us-west and us-east (from their server list). The corresponding two server host (gateway) addresses: eth-us-east.flexpool.io and eth-us-west.flexpool.io have their IP addresses pointing to Ashburn in Virginia and Portland in Oregon respectively.
We assume that the service gateway should be close to the mining server locations to reduce latencies, and we use these two gateway locations as the locations of the mining rigs. This is an educated guess.
For each reporting region, we attribute the hashrate equally to the countries included in that reporting regions. This is the best guess we can get without further information.

Assumptions and limitations

The calculation of Ethereum network emission follows the same logic as for Bitcoin with the following additions:
  • There is no reliable/trustworthy source for historical Ethereum hashrate distribution data.
  • We build tools and processes to estimating daily hashrate distribution with hashrate share of a selected set of pools and their geolocations.
Within the energy consumption calculation:
  • The trend line on plotted data does incorporate both the most profitable/efficient hardware and the least efficient/ profitable hardware. However, this could potentially be skewed upwards - since miners (industrial and hobbyists) may not necessarily use the most efficient mining rigs. The individual who published the paper has analysed some data to verify that a spread of both the most efficient and least efficient hardware has been used (Figure 3 in calculating emissions for Ethereum). However, this exercise was only done for a short period (for 5 days between 2021-10 and 2021-11) and was only completed on NanoPool’s mining pool. There may be issues, for example, the mining rig A10 has a hashrate efficiency of ~0.6 MH/s/W. This is plotted and including in the mining rate hash efficiency over time, and therefore included in the trend line. However, this is not present in the analysis of mining rigs actually used on NanoPool. Therefore, we may be incorporating rigs such as these which would not actually be used to mine Ethereum.
  • Furthermore, since the graph actually plots release date against hashrate efficiency - it seems to not account for any lag between when a rig is released and when it is actually incorporated into mining pool operations. Therefore, we could be further underestimating energy consumption as a result.
  • This methodology does not yet include embedded carbon emissions from the manufacture and disposal of the computers used as validator nodes.
