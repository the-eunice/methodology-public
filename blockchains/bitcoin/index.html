Bitcoin Carbon Footprint Methodology

The emissions due to holding and transacting Bitcoin (BTC) are calculated using the methodology outlined here, with the following modifications, data sources, and assumptions.

Electricity Consumption

We get total electricity consumption per day from the Cambridge Bitcoin Electricity Consumption Index (CBECI).
The CBECI data provides daily figures estimating the hourly electricity demand for the whole of the bitcoin network, so we multiply by 24 to get the daily electricity usage.

Carbon emissions

The geographic distribution of the Bitcoin network’s mining nodes is provided by the CBECI Mining Map, which is created from data submitted by CBECI’s Bitcoin mining partners.
Given this geographic breakdown, we use emissions factors for grid-purchased electricity in the various countries to calculate the total carbon emissions due to the electricity consumption of the Bitcoin network.
The emissions factors for grid-purchased electricity in the respective countries come from various national and multi-national government sources including the US EPA, and the European AIB, as well as climate NGOs such as Greenhouse Gas Protocol, and Climate Transparency.

Blockchain Metrics

Blockchain metrics are provided by Messari, including:
  • Daily circulating supply of Bitcoin
  • Bitcoin daily transaction fees
  • Bitcoin daily block rewards paid to miners

Assumptions and limitations

  • We are assuming that the hourly electricity demand figures provided by CBECI are representative of a whole day.
  • Bitcoin hashrate data is provided for individual months, but country emissions factors for purchased electricity are provided as a single value for a whole year, so we use the same emissions factor for all the months in the year.
  • CBECI monthly hashrate data lags the current date by several months. For any months after the most recent hashrate data is available, we assume the hashrate geographic distribution stays the same.
  • This methodology assumes that all Bitcoin mining uses electricity purchased from the national grid, and therefore has the same emissions profile as the grid. This is likely to over-estimate emissions, since many miners will co-locate with electricity generators providing the cheapest electricity in order to get the best price. These are often renewable energy generators (since that’s the cheapest electricity available) who are unable to sell all of their production to the local grid. We are sourcing data to get more clarity on the extent to which this is happening, so that we can adjust our emissions calculation to provide a more realistic figure.
  • A certain amount of Bitcoin mining is powered using waste methane from sources such as flared gas from oil drilling operations, and collecting emitted methane from landfills and farms. This activity will have a net negative carbon footprint, since it lowers the amount of methane which would otherwise be emitted into the atmosphere[^1]. This methodology does not account for this carbon negative impact of Bitcoin mining because we have not yet been able to gather reliable data about the real extent and impact of this activity.
  • This methodology does not yet include embedded carbon emissions from the manufacture and disposal of the hardware used for Bitcoin mining.
[^1]: Methane is a much more potent greenhouse gas than CO2. Flaring natural gas (which is mostly methane), for example when it is an unwanted byproduct of oil drilling, leaves most of the methane unburned and venting into the atmosphere. Burning this gas in a modern turbine combusts almost all of the methane and emits waste CO2 instead. Since CO2 is a less potent greenhouse gas, the net effect of this is lower emissions than flaring the gas or, in the case of landfills and farms, letting it vent directly into the air.
