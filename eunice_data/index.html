Eunice Carbon Footprint Methodology

This document describes the Eunice methodology for calculating the carbon emissions due to holding and trading cryptocurrencies.
This is the general outline of the methodology. The specifics of the calculations for each individual cryptocurrency are documented in their respective README files.

Cryptocurrency Emissions

The Eunice API provides emissions in kgCO2e[^1] for holding and transacting different cryptocurrencies over time.

To calculate emissions due to holding and transacting cryptocurrencies, we need the emissions factors for those activities, for each cryptocurrency.
To determine those values, we take the total greenhouse gas emissions from activity required to maintain the blockchain network[^2], and apportion that to both holders and transacters using a modified version of the ‘hybrid’ (i.e. factoring in both holding and transacting cryptocurrency) methodology developed by South Pole and later modified by the Crypto Carbon Ratings Institute. This methodology splits responsibility for emissions between holders and transacters based on the percentage of miner/validator revenue which comes from the block reward versus transaction fees.

Electricity Consumption

To derive the total electricity consumption of a blockchain network, we can use either a “top down” or a “bottom up” approach.
The top down calculation is usually used for proof of work (PoW) networks such as bitcoin, where the total hashpower of the network is readily available. Given the total hashpower, we can derive electricity usage based on manufacturer data on the power consumption and calculation efficiency of popular mining hardware.

For proof of stake (PoS) networks, where hashpower is not a factor, a bottom up approach estimates the typical hardware we can expect network nodes to consist of. This will be influenced by the published requirements for CPU, memory, and storage which are always provided as a part of the cryptocurrency’s official documentation.
Given the hardware profile of a typical node, we can find the electricity consumption for such a computer from manufacturer data, and multiply by the number of network nodes to get the total network electricity consumption. Number of nodes is a readily available parameter for all public proof of stake blockchain networks.

Carbon Emissions

Given the total electricity consumption of a blockchain network, we derive the CO2-equivalent carbon emissions in kilograms of CO2 equivalent (kgCO2e) by applying emissions factors for the electricity consumed.
Different electricity sources result in different amounts of emissions per megawatt hour - solar energy emits very little, while coal-fired power stations emit a great deal. So, we need to work out the emissions factor(s) to use for the blockchain network’s electricity consumption.
We do this based on the geographic distribution by country of the blockchain nodes, and the published emissions factor for purchased electricity in the relevant country, modified to try to account for ‘behind the meter’ electricity deals.
Node distribution can be estimated from the IP addresses of inter-node communications such as block publication announcements. This is imperfect, since IP addresses can be ‘spoofed’ to conceal the true origin of a message, and the ‘private by design’ nature of some blockchains means that not every message will contain reliable origin data. For example, the IP number in a bitcoin block publication announcement is the address of the announcing node, but that isn’t necessarily the node which actually mined the block. Nevertheless, this is the best available source of geographic distribution data for blockchain nodes.
For PoW miners, electricity is likely to represent at least 80% of their operating costs, so larger installations will often have separate agreements with electricity generators and grid operators, buying wholesale electricity rather than paying the fluctuating market spot price. The larger the mining installation, the more likely it is that they will have special arrangements, so using the host country’s purchased electricity emissions factor risks significantly overestimating the blockchain’s carbon footprint.
To mitigate this we derive a modification factor by extrapolating information provided by our miner contacts about their electricity purchase agreements and sources.

Share of Total Blockchain Emissions

Once we know the total carbon emissions for a blockchain network in kgCO2e, we can apportion them to users of the blockchain network.
In our methodology, both holding and transacting cryptocurrencies incur emissions, and we split the total emissions between holders and transacters based on the percentage of miner/validator revenue that comes from the block reward versus transaction fees.

Holding cryptocurrency

For holders of a cryptocurrency, their share of the emissions depends on the value of their holdings as a fraction of the total circulating supply of blockchain tokens.[^3]
For transacters, their share of the emissions depends on the total fees paid for the transactions they make as a fraction of the total transaction fees on the blockchain network.

Non-native tokens

On some blockchains users can hold and transact value in non-native crypto tokens. An example would be ERC20 tokens on the Ethereum blockchain, which can represent other cryptocurrencies such as WBTC (“wrapped” Bitcoin), fiat currencies such as USDC or USDT, or tokenised representations of financial instruments like derivatives, or off-chain stocks.
In the Eunice methodology, we treat these non-native tokens as if they were the equivalent value of the native cryptocurrency, for the purpose of calculating their emissions due to holding the token.
For example, if a user is holding WETH tokens, which are wrapped ETH and equivalent in value to ETH, then holding 100 WETH incurs exactly the same emissions as holding 100 ETH. If the user is holding 10,000 USDC, and the USDC/ETH exchange rate on the relevant date is 1,000 USDC/ETH, then the emissions due to holding 10,000 USDC are the same as the emissions from holding 10 ETH, and so on.
This methodology allows us to easily calculate the value of the holding emissions, even when the tokens represent cryptocurrency belonging to a different blockchain. For example, it is common to bridge Bitcoin (BTC) to other networks to use it with decentralized finance protocols.
This ensures that we calculate reasonable values for the emissions due to holding cryptocurrency, regardless of the specific token in which the value is held.

Non-native tokens

Non-native token transactions do not require any special treatment, using the Eunice methodology. ERC20 transactions are simply handled the same way as any other transactions, in terms of calculating their emissions footprint.

[^1]: kgCO2e is a measure of greenhouse gas emissions. It stands for kilograms of CO2 equivalent.
[^2]: Using Bitcoin as an example, this would be the total greenhouse gas emissions of all Bitcoin mining activity on a given date, since mining is the activity which enables transactions and secures the bitcoin network.
[^3]: Currently, our methodology does not adjust for “lost” tokens, such as those where the owner has lost their wallet private key, rendering the tokens unusable.
